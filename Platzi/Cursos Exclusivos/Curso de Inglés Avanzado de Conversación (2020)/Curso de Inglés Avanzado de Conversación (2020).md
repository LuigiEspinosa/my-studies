## The 3 Ps... and the Overlap

The 3 Ps = **Presentation, Presenting and Participating**.

Most situations are not just one or the other. They overlap, or are very similar for both situations. They go in between being formal and informal, so you have to deal with both of those.

**Business Savvy: Formal**

_They..._

- May have learned English at school.
- Are very formal
- Knows all of the words for their job
- Do very well at meetings where they have to explain charts and news lots of formal words

_But also they..._

- Can't handle a regular conversation properly
- Haven't learned any slang
- May sound very robotic

**Discussion Ready: Informal**

_They..._

- May've learned English from their friends or the Internet
- Are very informal
- Know all of the slang
- Can have a really nice conversation
- Are a little bit too informal for work
- Say "you guys", "ain't", different things like that in a business meeting

**Middle Ground: Clever Hybrid**

- They're an expert on formal and informal situations
- and prepare to deal with 21st century business situations

### Informal Situation: Small Talk

> "Making small talk is a **way to** contact and **connect** with people and intends to offer you contacts... **that last for a longer time period.**" *- Chitra Reddy, content.wisestep.com*

**What topics are ok to talk about?**

- Introduce Yourself.
- Ask questions AND listen.
- Share details about yourself.

**What topics are NOT ok to talk about?**

- Do all the talking.
- Ask questions WITHOUT listening.
- Share TOO much.

**When you do it correctly, small talk can:**

- Be fun.
- Help you make new connections.
- Learn more about other cultures.

### 3 Ways to Practice

- Alone.
	- Timing.
	- Audio.
	- Video.
	- Take Notes.
	- Repeat.

- With a Partner.
	- Record Yourself.
	- Partner can give you suggestions.
	- You can learn form their style.

- With a Coach.
	- Record Yourself.
	- Fastest Progress.
	- Learn New Techniques.

## P #2: Presenting

### Earn Respect

- Avoid Filler Words.
- Appropriate Eye Contact.

> *Pregnant Pauses always gave birth to new ideas.*

### Comparisons

Talking about the differences AND similarities between two things, people or places. 3 common words for comparisons:

- Than.
- Like.
- As.

![[Comparisons - Negative Description.png]]

### Keep it Moving

- Transition Words.
- Rhetorical Questions.

> "When you're linking ideas together, transitions are what help you lead the reader \[or the listener\] from one idea to the next" *- Lisa McLendon, The Perfect English Grammar Workbook*.

**6 types of Transition Words**

- Addition or Similarity (In addition).
- Contrast or Opposite (However).
- Examples or Emphasis (For instance).
- Results or Effects (As a result).
- Time or Sequence (Meanwhile).
- Conclusion or Summary (To sum up).

## P #3: Participating

### Be a Good Listener

*You heard... But are you listening?*

**Avoid**

- Looking at your device.
- Any multi-tasking.
- Hearing just to reply.
- Don't just stare like a maniac.

**Please**

- Listen without interrupting.
- Look at the person.
- Ask questions to show you are listening.

There are no stupid questions Take mental notes in your head.

### Ask Insightful Questions

**GET MORE INFO**, Avoid 'Yes' or 'No' questions.

Informal: 'I have an iguana'.

- How long have you had it?
- What do they eat?
- Why did you decide to get an iguana?

Formal: 'Here is the quarterly report'.

- What does this acronym mean?
- You mentioned... how does affect...?

### Give Feedback

Build up, don't tear down. Consider STTIP (Setting, Time, Tools, Ingredients, Practice).

**Praise and Polish** \[praise -> polish\]

- I really like the content but I felt like you weren't loud enough.

**Sandwich Method** \[praise -> polish -> praise\]

- I really like the content, but we'd like you to be louder. But overall, it was very good because we know you worked on the timing.

### Get Feedback

Not an attack, meant to help you improve (most of the time).

- Triple A (Agree, Answer, Action).
- Ask for clarification.

## The Overlap

### Transition

> "High self-monitors are like "social chameleons". They... present different "versions" of themselves, depending on the situation. They blend in." *- Ronald E. Riggio, PhD, Psychology Today*

### Informal to Formal

- Informal: Can.
	- Hey, can you come to the party later?
	- Sorry, I can't make it today.

- Formal: Able to.
	- Hello, are you able to work late this evening?
	- Sorry, I'm not able to today, but I can later this week.

---

- When your partner/relative is your workmate or boss.
- Talking with a workmate versus your boss.
- Lunch meeting versus conference call.

### Formal to Informal

- Formal: Spell it out.
	- If you **are not** able to attend, please email.
	- The client **should not** distribute this for commercial use.

- Informal: Contractions and Acronyms.
	- Hey, u coming ltr?
	- Sry, idk. Ttyl.
	- Please don't share this with anyone else.

### Help Others

- Don't Gossip.
- Be a Mentor.
- Allow them to self-correct.
	- Do you mean...?
	- Could you say that again?
- Lead like an Elephant.
	- Be Gentle.
	- Move Slowly.
	- There is no I in Team.

### Clarify the level of Formality

- Do you prefer Mr./Ms... or (1st Name)?
- Mirror the other person/people.
- You can call me (1st name).
- Body Language
